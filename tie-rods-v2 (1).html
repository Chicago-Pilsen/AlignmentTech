Component 01
Outer Tie Rod
Also known as tie rod end
1
Threaded Shaft
Adjustable threaded rod. Thread engagement controls toe adjustment — turning changes wheel alignment angle.
2
Lock / Jam Nut
Hex nut that locks the outer tie rod after alignment is set. Must be retorqued after any toe adjustment.
3
Ball Joint Housing
Articulating ball-and-socket assembly allowing multi-axis movement across suspension travel and steering input.
4
Dust Boot
Rubber bellows protecting the ball joint from contamination. Cracked boots accelerate joint failure significantly.
5
Tapered Stud & Cotter Pin
Morse-taper pin seats into the steering knuckle. Cotter pin prevents castle nut from backing off under vibration.
Component 02
Inner Tie Rod
Also known as rack end
1
Rack Thread Interface
Threaded end screws directly into rack housing. Often uses left-hand threads on driver side for self-tightening under load.
2
Hex Wrench Body
Large hex section provides purchase for install/removal. Requires special crowfoot or inner tie rod socket tool.
3
Rack Bellows Boot
Large accordion-style rubber boot seals the inner joint and rack housing, retaining steering rack lubricant.
4
Pivot Ball Joint
High-load ball-and-socket pivot directly handling steering forces from the rack. Higher load rating than outer joint.
5
Threaded Shaft to Outer
Accepts the outer tie rod end. Thread depth sets the baseline toe before outer end fine-tunes the measurement.

Rack & Knuckle Components

Extended System Reference
Component 03
Steering Knuckle
Also known as spindle or upright
1
Hub Bearing Assembly
Precision double-row angular contact bearing allowing wheel rotation while transmitting vertical, lateral, and axial loads to the knuckle body.
2
Outer Tie Rod Mounting Boss
Tapered bore accepts the outer tie rod stud. Steering geometry converges here — this point defines the instantaneous steering axis at the wheel.
3
Strut / Pinch Bolt Mount
Top of knuckle clamps around the strut tube via pinch bolt. Eccentric bolt or cam adjusters often provide camber correction range.
4
Lower Ball Joint Socket
Accepts lower control arm ball joint. Defines the lower pivot of the steering axis (KPI — king pin inclination) in combination with top strut mount.
5
Brake Caliper Mounting Ears
Cast bosses with threaded bores for sliding brake caliper bracket bolts. Knuckle absorbs all braking torque reaction forces.
Component 04
Rack & Pinion Assembly
Hydraulic or electric power assist
1
Pinion / Input Shaft
Connects to steering column via universal joint. Pinion gear meshes with rack teeth to convert rotational input to linear rack travel.
2
Rack Teeth & Housing Bore
Precision machined rack slides linearly within the housing. Rack travel per steering wheel turn is set by pinion gear ratio — typically 2.5–3 turns lock-to-lock.
3
EPAS / Assist Motor
Electric power assist motor (EPAS) applies torque directly to the rack or pinion. Replaces hydraulic systems on modern vehicles — no fluid, lower parasitic loss.
4
Inner Tie Rod Bellows Boots
Accordion rubber seals at each rack end protect the inner tie rod ball joints and retain rack lubricant. Torn boots lead to rapid inner tie rod failure.
5
Rack Mount Brackets
Bolted to subframe or chassis rails. Isolating bushings in brackets dampen road noise transmission into the cabin. Worn bushings allow rack movement under load.

Failure Symptoms

All Four Components

Outer Tie Rod

Uneven tire wear — feathering or inside/outside edge wear from persistent toe error
Loose or wandering steering at highway speeds — wheel "floats" on straight sections
Clunking or popping from front end during turns or low-speed bump impacts
Visible horizontal play when shaking tire in 9 & 3 o'clock position
Vehicle pulls to one side under braking — asymmetric toe geometry

Inner Tie Rod

Excessive steering dead zone — large rotation before road wheels respond
Rack bellows boot collapsed, torn, or filled with steering rack grease
Vibration or shimmy through steering wheel over rough pavement
Clunking felt through steering wheel during turns — transmits through rack
Rapid repeated outer tie rod wear after fresh alignments — inner failure masking root cause

Steering Knuckle

Grinding, rumbling or humming noise from wheel area — progressive wheel bearing failure
ABS light illuminated — bearing-integrated ABS sensor signal loss
Visible corrosion, cracking, or deformation at strut pinch bolt area after impact damage
Lateral wheel wobble at speed — severely worn hub bearing or damaged knuckle bore
Camber or caster won't align within spec after collision — bent knuckle casting

Rack & Pinion

Hydraulic fluid leak from boot area or hose fittings — power steering fluid on ground
Stiff or hard steering on one lock — internal rack wear causing tight spot
Whining or groaning from power steering pump — low fluid from rack leak
On-center free play — worn pinion preload adjuster, rack yoke, or pinion bearings
EPAS warning light or reduced assist — motor, torque sensor, or control module fault
